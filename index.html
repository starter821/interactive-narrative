Is Washington State Still Affordable?

If you've been grocery shopping lately, you may have noticed your cart feels a little lighter for the same $20. If you're renting, you know the feeling of opening a lease renewal and holding your breath. If you're working full-time and still watching your savings thin out, you're not imagining it, and you're not alone.

Over the past two decades, the cost of living in Washington State has climbed faster than most paychecks can follow. Housing, food, gas, and healthcare have all risen, and while wages have grown, they haven't kept up.

This piece follows that story through the numbers: what has gotten more expensive, who is feeling it most, and what Washington has begun to do about it.

Data note: CPI data is from the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics for the Seattle-Tacoma-Bellevue metro area. It measures price changes relative to each region's own base period. Data is bimonthly and not seasonally adjusted.

Inflation Trend

Prices in Washington State have risen dramatically since 2000. After a relatively steady increase through the 2000s and 2010s, inflation surged following the COVID-19 pandemic. Supply chain disruptions, increased consumer demand, and federal stimulus contributed to the sharp rise in prices between 2021 and 2023. Although Seattle is already one of the most expensive metro areas in the country, its overall inflation trend closely followed the national average. In Seattle, the cost of the same basket of goods is now more than double what it was in 2000.

Data note: Cumulative CPI Change is rebased to 2000. For example, 29% on June 2009, means that prices in June 2009 were 29% higher than in June 2000. Inflation rate of 4.2% in the same month, means that prices increased by 4.2% compared to the previous year (June 2008).

Not All Prices Rise Equally

While the overall Consumer Price Index shows the broad trend, the categories people rely on every day tell a more uneven story.

Since 2000, housing costs in Seattle have risen steadily, climbing far faster than most other categories. Medical care has also increased consistently over time, continuing upward regardless of recessions or short term economic swings. The most noticeable increase is gas, with a cumulative CPI of 315.7% in August 2023. These are expenses households cannot easily cut back on. Rent must still be paid, and healthcare remains necessary even when budgets tighten. Apparel stands in sharp contrast. Prices have remained relatively flat for much of the past 25 years, and at times even declined. Altogether, these trends show that inflation does not affect all goods and services equally. The largest increases have occurred in essentials like housing, healthcare, and gas, categories that make up a significant share of spending for low and moderate income households.

How the $20 Grocery Cart Changed from 2006 to 2026

A $20 grocery budget goes much less far today than it did in 2006. This visualization tracks how rising food prices in Washington State changed a typical grocery basket over two decades. While staples like bread, milk, and eggs remain, more expensive items such as beef and fresh produce gradually disappear. The shrinking basket shows how inflation affects food choices, nutrition and everyday quality of life.

Rent Cost Burden

In Washington State, more renters are spending over half of their income on rent. From 2010 to 2022, the number of severely cost-burdened renter households rose from about 430,000 to more than 520,000.

But the pressure is not shared equally. Renters earning less than 80% of HAMFI make up the vast majority of affected households every year, usually around 85% to 90% of the total. Middle- and higher-income renters appear in the data, but their shares remain much smaller.

Washington’s rent crisis is growing, but it is still hitting low-income renters the hardest. The data points to an urgent need for deeply affordable housing and stronger support for lower-income households.

Growth vs. Inflation

Washington State’s economy has become significantly more productive over the past 25 years, but worker pay has not kept pace. Using 1999 as a baseline, this visualization compares growth in productivity, real wages, and consumer prices. By 2025, productivity had increased by more than 50%, while real pay rose by only about 30%. Meanwhile, prices climbed nearly 90%, driven by inflation in recent years. The widening gap highlights a growing challenge for workers: even as the economy produces more, rising living costs continue to outpace wage growth.

When housing and basic expenses rise faster than paychecks, states respond in two directions: reduce what households pay, or increase what households keep.
Reduce What Households Pay: Washington Rent Growth Slows as New Cap Takes Effect

Average rents in Washington State have continued to climb, surpassing $2,000 per month by 2025. However, rent growth has slowed sharply from the double-digit increases seen during the pandemic housing boom. This visualization compares rising rent levels with annual growth rates and the new statewide rent cap established under HB 1217. As the policy takes effect in 2025, it marks a new effort to limit future rent increases and improve housing affordability for renters.

Increase What Households Keep: Working Families Tax Credit

Washington State created the Working Families Tax Credit (WFTC) in 2008 to help offset the tax burden on low-income workers. Modeled after the federal Earned Income Tax Credit, the program was designed to return some sales tax revenue to low- and moderate-income households. Because Washington does not have a state income tax, lower-income residents often pay a larger share of their income through sales taxes. Although the law passed in 2008, the program went unfunded for more than a decade. During that time, Washington experienced the 2008 financial crisis, rising housing costs, and increasing living expenses. In 2021, as inflation and living costs surged following the COVID-19 pandemic, the legislature finally funded the credit. The first payments were distributed in 2023. Today, the program is managed by the Washington State Department of Revenue and aims to support the financial stability of low- and moderate-income residents across the state.

Is Washington Still Affordable?

For many residents, the answer is no, not the way it used to be.

Prices have more than doubled since 2000. Rent now averages over $2,000 a month statewide, and nearly one in four renter households spends more than half their income on housing. Wages have grown, but not fast enough to keep pace.

Washington has taken steps to respond. A new rent cap limits annual increases, and the Working Families Tax Credit puts money back into the hands of lower-income households. Both are meaningful. Neither is enough on its own.

Affordability is shaped by policy choices made each legislative session in Olympia. For Washington residents, those choices are worth watching.

Take action: Washington residents who qualify for the Working Families Tax Credit can apply through the Washington State Department of Revenue. Many eligible households have not yet applied.
