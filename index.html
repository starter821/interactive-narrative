Is Washington State Still Affordable?

Over the past several years, Washington State has experienced a sharp increase in the cost of living driven by inflation, rising housing prices, supply chain disruptions, and broader economic changes. Although wages have increased during this period, many workers have struggled to keep up with rising costs for housing, food, transportation, and other necessities. This article analyzes trends in inflation, wages, and living costs in Washington State and examines how these economic changes have affected affordability and influenced policy discussions surrounding wages and cost of living.

Data note: CPI data is from the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics for the Seattle-Tacoma-Bellevue metro area. It measures price changes relative to each region's own base period. Data is bimonthly and not seasonally adjusted.

Viz3: How the $20 Grocery Cart Changed from 2006 to 2026

A $20 grocery budget goes much less far today than it did in 2006. This visualization tracks how rising food prices in Washington State changed a typical grocery basket over two decades. While staples like bread, milk, and eggs remain, more expensive items such as beef and fresh produce gradually disappear. The shrinking basket shows how inflation affects food choices, nutrition and everyday quality of life.

Viz4: Rent Cost Burden

In Washington State, more renters are spending over half of their income on rent. From 2010 to 2022, the number of severely cost-burdened renter households rose from about 430,000 to more than 520,000.

But the pressure is not shared equally. Renters earning less than 80% of HAMFI make up the vast majority of affected households every year, usually around 85% to 90% of the total. Middle- and higher-income renters appear in the data, but their shares remain much smaller.

Washington’s rent crisis is growing, but it is still hitting low-income renters the hardest. The data points to an urgent need for deeply affordable housing and stronger support for lower-income households.

Viz5: Wage Growth vs. Inflation

Washington State’s economy has become significantly more productive over the past 25 years, but worker pay has not kept pace. Using 1999 as a baseline, this visualization compares growth in productivity, real wages, and consumer prices. By 2025, productivity had increased by more than 50%, while real pay rose by only about 30%. Meanwhile, prices climbed nearly 90%, driven by inflation in recent years. The widening gap highlights a growing challenge for workers: even as the economy produces more, rising living costs continue to outpace wage growth.

When housing and basic expenses rise faster than paychecks, states respond in two directions: reduce what households pay, or increase what households keep.
Viz6: Washington Rent Growth Slows as New Cap Takes Effect

Average rents in Washington State have continued to climb, surpassing $2,000 per month by 2025. However, rent growth has slowed sharply from the double-digit increases seen during the pandemic housing boom. This visualization compares rising rent levels with annual growth rates and the new statewide rent cap established under HB 1217. As the policy takes effect in 2025, it marks a new effort to limit future rent increases and improve housing affordability for renters.

Viz7: Working Families Tax Credit
Washington State created the Working Families Tax Credit (WFTC) in 2008 to help offset the tax burden on low-income workers. Modeled after the federal Earned Income Tax Credit, the program was designed to return some sales tax revenue to low- and moderate-income households. Because Washington does not have a state income tax, lower-income residents often pay a larger share of their income through sales taxes. Although the law passed in 2008, the program went unfunded for more than a decade. During that time, Washington experienced the 2008 financial crisis, rising housing costs, and increasing living expenses. In 2021, as inflation and living costs surged following the COVID-19 pandemic, the legislature finally funded the credit. The first payments were distributed in 2023. Today, the program is managed by the Washington State Department of Revenue and aims to support the financial stability of low- and moderate-income residents across the state.
