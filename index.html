Is Washington State Still Affordable?
Over the past several years, Washington State has experienced a sharp increase in the cost of living driven by inflation, rising housing prices, supply chain disruptions, and broader economic changes. Although wages have increased during this period, many workers have struggled to keep up with rising costs for housing, food, transportation, and other necessities. As a result, the gap between income and basic living expenses has widened for many residents. This article analyzes trends in inflation, wages, and living costs in Washington State and examines how these economic changes have affected affordability and influenced policy discussions surrounding wages and cost of living.

Important Notes About the Data: The Seattle data is sourced from the Consumer Price Index (CPI) published by the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) for the Seattle-Tacoma-Bellevue metropolitan area. The CPI measures average changes in prices for a fixed basket of goods and services over time. CPI data does not represent absolute price levels across cities, but rather changes in prices within each region relative to its own base period. The data is bimonthly and not seasonally adjusted, so short-term fluctuations may reflect seasonal effects.

Viz7: Working Families Tax Credit
Washington State created the Working Families Tax Credit (WFTC) in 2008 to help offset the tax burden on low-income workers. Modeled after the federal Earned Income Tax Credit, the program was designed to return some sales tax revenue to low- and moderate-income households. Because Washington does not have a state income tax, lower-income residents often pay a larger share of their income through sales taxes. Although the law passed in 2008, the program went unfunded for more than a decade. During that time, Washington experienced the 2008 financial crisis, rising housing costs, and increasing living expenses. In 2021, as inflation and living costs surged following the COVID-19 pandemic, the legislature finally funded the credit. The first payments were distributed in 2023. Today, the program is managed by the Washington State Department of Revenue and aims to support the financial stability of low- and moderate-income residents across the state.
